Welcome to the Oppia Tutorial! We will help you get started with your first exploration. First, we'll go over basic definitions and best practices. Then we'll end by making a small exploration.

Exploration Editor

The first thing you see when you click on "Create an Exploration," and fill out the initial information, is the following page, the Exploration Editor.

Oppia will start you off with a State called First State. States are important because they are the building blocks for your explorations. Oppia is based on States. You can think of States as the places a learner (anybody who views your exploration) can potentially reach. All of your States are visible in the Exploration Overview (also in the picture). The interface will only show one state at a time, and you can navigate to your other States by clicking on them in the Exploration Overview box.

Components of a State

Simply put, a State is just some text along with a question (interaction) and some rules that can take the learner to another State depending on their answer. These are the technical terms and definitions:

Content

The editable block of text you see in the picture. Write whatever you want the learner to read.

Interaction

This could be a text field or any kind of input field, even a map. A learner will interact with these elements by putting in some text or placing a marker on a map.

Rule

Once an interaction is chosen, you can choose what happens according to what a learner inputs. From here, you can provide feedback and then have the learner redo the interaction or move them to a different state.

Note Rules are where you can specify a new state for the learner to go to. You can't do it from the Content on the Interaction.

Typical Use Case

You will start off with a State. After you add some content, you can add an interaction, and then add one or more rules. When you create a Rule you will specify the destination, which will either be the current state (if you want the learner to redo the interaction) or a new state (if you want to move the learner forward). Read on to see a concrete example.

What should an exploration be about?

Typically, it will revolve around a question. Usually, the more specific, the better. A few examples are:

  • What is the slope of a line?
  • What are the nutritional benefits of a banana?
  • What is the difference between Python and C#?

Note The exploration does not need to go in depth or cover all the aspects of what you are trying to teach. The goal is to make something basic, and then expand on it once learners start using the exploration (because you'll have access to their answers and can use that to add more content/interactions/rules).
